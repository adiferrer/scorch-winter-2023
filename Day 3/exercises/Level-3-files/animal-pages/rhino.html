Rhino

Least Concern

Rhinos are large, herbivorous mammals that are native to Africa and Asia. There are five species of rhinos: the white rhino and black rhino in Africa, and the Indian rhino, Javan rhino, and Sumatran rhino in Asia.

Rhinos have a thick, armored skin and two horns made of keratin, the same material as human hair and nails. Rhinos use their horns for defense against predators and to establish dominance during mating season. Unfortunately, this has also made them a target for poaching, as their horns are highly valued in some cultures for their supposed medicinal properties.

Rhinos are herbivores, feeding on a variety of vegetation including grasses, leaves, and shoots. They have a prehensile upper lip, which they use to grasp and pull food into their mouths. Rhinos are large and powerful animals, with some species weighing over 2,000 pounds. They are also surprisingly fast and can run up to 30 miles per hour.

Rhinos are threatened by habitat loss, poaching, and human conflict. Conservation efforts are underway to protect rhino populations and their habitats, including programs to reduce poaching, establish protected areas, and translocate rhinos to areas with suitable habitat. Rhinos are also popular animals for zoos and wildlife parks, contributing to their conservation and education.
